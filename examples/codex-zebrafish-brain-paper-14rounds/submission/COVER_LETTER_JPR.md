# Draft Cover Letter for Journal of Proteome Research

Dear Editors of *Journal of Proteome Research*,

Please consider our manuscript, **"Distinct Proteoform Regimes in Adult Zebrafish Telencephalon and Optic Tectum,"** for publication as an Article in *Journal of Proteome Research*.

This manuscript uses a published spatially resolved top-down proteomics pilot study of adult zebrafish telencephalon and optic tectum to make a sharper biological claim than the original narrative presentation made explicit. Rather than presenting a new mass spectrometry acquisition, the paper asks whether the published counts and released source tables already support a clear, biologically coherent regional proteoform architecture. We show that they do. The two regions share only 35 proteoforms out of a union of 807 at the article level, yielding a proteoform-level Jaccard overlap of 0.0434; the released exact-ID tables remain in the same low-overlap regime and fall far below marginal-prevalence expectations. The named marker families discussed in the source study also align strongly with the expected region, with a marker alignment fraction of 0.9766 and an exact-test odds ratio of 1173.

We believe the manuscript is a good fit for *Journal of Proteome Research* for three reasons. First, it is centered on proteoform-resolved interpretation of a published top-down proteomics experiment. Second, it contributes a reproducible quantitative framework for turning a small spatial proteomics pilot into a new, explicit knowledge claim about regional molecular organization rather than a primarily narrative result. Third, recent work in the journal has shown that *JPR* values method development, transparent computational analysis, and resource-style studies that make proteomics evidence more reusable. Our manuscript is aligned with that tradition, while staying honest about scope: it is a public-data discovery and analysis paper, not a claim of new wet-lab acquisition.

To make the work maximally inspectable, we have assembled the underlying code, curated evidence tables, figure-generation scripts, and analysis outputs into a coherent local package. The paper therefore aims to contribute not only a biological interpretation, but also a model for how published small-scale spatial proteomics studies can be made easier to audit and extend.

This manuscript has not been published elsewhere and is not under consideration by another journal. All authors have approved the manuscript and agree with its submission to *Journal of Proteome Research*.
