# Draft Cover Letter for Journal of Proteome Research

Dear Editors of *Journal of Proteome Research*,

Please consider our manuscript, **"Quantitative Re-analysis of Published Top-Down Proteoform Counts Reveals Regional Separation in the Adult Zebrafish Brain,"** for publication as an Article in *Journal of Proteome Research*.

This manuscript revisits a published spatially resolved top-down proteomics pilot study of adult zebrafish telencephalon and optic tectum and converts the reported proteoform counts into a compact quantitative re-analysis. Rather than presenting a new mass spectrometry acquisition, the paper asks a narrower question: do the published counts support a clear and biologically coherent regional separation, and can that claim be made more explicit and auditable than in the source article? We show that they do. The two regions share only 35 proteoforms out of a union of 807, yielding a proteoform-level Jaccard overlap of 0.0434, and the named marker families discussed in the source study align strongly with the expected region, with a marker alignment fraction of 0.9766 and an exact-test odds ratio of 1173.

We believe the manuscript is a good fit for *Journal of Proteome Research* for three reasons. First, it is centered on proteoform-resolved interpretation of a published top-down proteomics experiment. Second, it contributes a reproducible quantitative framework for turning a small spatial proteomics pilot into an inspectable regional comparison rather than a primarily narrative result. Third, recent work in the journal has shown that *JPR* values method development, transparent computational analysis, and resource-style studies that make proteomics evidence more reusable. Our manuscript is aligned with that tradition, while staying honest about scope: it is a reproducible re-analysis article, not a claim of new wet-lab discovery.

To make the work maximally inspectable, we have assembled the underlying code, curated evidence tables, figure-generation scripts, and analysis outputs into a coherent local package. The paper therefore aims to contribute not only a biological interpretation, but also a model for how published small-scale spatial proteomics studies can be made easier to audit and extend.

This manuscript has not been published elsewhere and is not under consideration by another journal. All authors have approved the manuscript and agree with its submission to *Journal of Proteome Research*.
