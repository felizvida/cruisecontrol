# Draft Cover Letter for Journal of Proteome Research

Dear Editors of *Journal of Proteome Research*,

Please consider our manuscript, **"Distinct Proteoform Regimes in Adult Zebrafish Telencephalon and Optic Tectum,"** for publication as an Article in *Journal of Proteome Research*.

This manuscript presents a proteoform-resolved comparison of adult zebrafish telencephalon and optic tectum using released top-down proteomics tables from a laser-capture microdissection capillary-zone-electrophoresis tandem mass spectrometry study. The central result is that the two regions occupy sharply distinct proteoform regimes rather than lightly shifted versions of the same local proteome. At the article level the regions share 35 proteoforms out of a union of 807, corresponding to a Jaccard overlap of 0.0434. In the released exact-ID tables, the shared overlap remains comparably low, and the observed overlap falls far below fixed-margin prevalence expectations. A curated marker panel and an independent literature-derived composition panel both align strongly with the expected regional biology.

We believe the manuscript is a strong fit for *Journal of Proteome Research* for three reasons. First, it is centered on proteoforms rather than protein-level summaries, which places the work directly in the journal's core measurement layer. Second, it turns a publicly released top-down dataset into a quantitative and reproducible regional comparison by combining exact-ID matching, canonicalized matching, protein-collapsed views, abundance-weighted similarities, prevalence-adjusted overlap tests, and sensitivity analyses. Third, the paper is built as an inspectable proteomics resource: code, traceability tables, figure-generation scripts, and source-data manifests are packaged alongside the manuscript so that the calculations can be checked and reused.

The manuscript keeps its claim bounded. It does not present a new acquisition campaign or a raw-file reprocessing workflow. Instead, it shows that the released top-down record already supports a specific biological conclusion: adult zebrafish telencephalon and optic tectum are strongly separated at the proteoform level, and that separation follows known regional function. The post-translational-modification follow-up is handled with the same discipline; a possible acetylation asymmetry weakens under detectability-aware sensitivity analysis and is therefore presented as hypothesis-generating rather than definitive.

We think this combination of proteoform focus, transparent computation, and biologically interpretable regional analysis matches the kind of method-aware application papers that *Journal of Proteome Research* publishes well.

This manuscript has not been published elsewhere and is not under consideration by another journal. All authors have approved the manuscript and agree with its submission to *Journal of Proteome Research*.
