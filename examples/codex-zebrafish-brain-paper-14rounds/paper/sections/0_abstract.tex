Adult zebrafish telencephalon and optic tectum support distinct forebrain and visuomotor functions, but small spatial proteomics studies do not always quantify that regional split explicitly. Here, we reanalyze the published top-down proteomics counts from a 2022 laser-capture microdissection-capillary zone electrophoresis-tandem mass spectrometry pilot study of those two regions and convert them into a compact reproducible comparison. The published counts reveal strong regional separation: the two regions share only 35 proteoforms out of a union of 807, yielding a proteoform-level Jaccard overlap of 0.0434, while the protein-level overlap fraction is 0.2151. We then test whether the marker families emphasized in the source paper are concentrated in the biologically expected region. Telencephalon-associated markers contribute 23 matched counts versus 2 spillover counts, whereas optic-tectum-associated markers contribute 102 matched counts versus 1 spillover count, corresponding to a marker alignment fraction of 0.9766 and a directional exact-test odds ratio of 1173 (one-sided $p = 5.12\times10^{-22}$). Because the curated marker panel represents only 15.20\% of the total regional count mass, we frame the strongest functional claim narrowly around that interpretable subset rather than the entire detected proteome. We additionally quantify the post-translational modification layer reported in the source study, including 358 modified proteoforms and 211 N-terminally acetylated species. This work does not report a new mass-spectrometry acquisition or full raw-data reprocessing pipeline; instead, it shows that a modest published spatial proteomics pilot can support a much clearer and more auditable regional-function argument when its reported counts are re-expressed as explicit quantitative evidence.
