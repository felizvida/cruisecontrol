# Literature Gap Audit

## Purpose

This memo records the places where the current Frederick paper still outruns its historiography. The point is not to enlarge the bibliography for its own sake. The point is to identify which claims still need a thicker scholarly base, then match those claims to the best secondary works and archival leads now in hand.

The current paper is no longer thin on Wilkinson himself. It now has a workable set of Wilkinson biography, Burr-crisis scholarship, and early-republic reputation literature. The remaining gaps are narrower and more important:

1. early republic military justice and court-martial procedure
2. Madison, Eustis, and War Department administrative context
3. Frederick legal culture, especially around Roger Brooke Taney
4. Frederick County local texture beyond the present marker-and-memory layer

## Critical Gaps After Round 30

### 1. Military Justice and Court-Martial Procedure

The paper makes a strong procedural claim: Frederick was one of the places where suspicion was turned into venue, evidence, testimony, judgment, and acquittal. The primary chain now supports that claim well. What is still light is the wider scholarship explaining how unusual, ordinary, or politically charged that process was.

### 2. Madison, Eustis, and War Department Administration

The paper is strongest when it treats Frederick as an administrative and political site, not just a courtroom location. It already shows that Madison and Eustis recognized and supplied the Frederick tribunal. What it still lacks is deeper scholarship on how early republic military administration worked, and why executive restraint in such a case mattered.

## Still Missing

The hardest remaining gap is not general Wilkinson literature. It is Frederick-specific legal and newspaper evidence from the 1811-1812 moment.

The next archival frontier is clear:

1. the printed 1811 court-martial report
2. Taney family and legal papers
3. Frederick newspapers
4. Maryland county judicial records

If those materials yield well, the next revision can make the Frederick section less cautious and more original without overclaiming.
