\begin{abstract}
James Wilkinson's long survival in national office has usually been told as a story of intrigue, nerve, or presidential indulgence. The Frederick record points to something more concrete. It shows how executive confidence was translated into venue, counsel, witnesses, interrogatories, and finally acquittal. Read together, the Jefferson--Wilkinson correspondence, a 1800 War Department receipt from Frederick Town, the 1808 presidential message on Wilkinson's inquiry, the 1811 Frederick court-martial letters, and Madison's approval of the acquittal reveal Frederick not as incidental scenery but as one of the places where scandal was given legal and administrative form. Jefferson's importance remains central, but not because he simply trusted Wilkinson as a friend. His support mattered because it kept Wilkinson within reach of formal vindication long enough for the case to be processed. Frederick mattered because that processing happened there. The town was one of the working sites at which political protection became procedure and procedure became survival.
\end{abstract}
