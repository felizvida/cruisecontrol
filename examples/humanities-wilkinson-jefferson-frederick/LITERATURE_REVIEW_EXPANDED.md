# Expanded Literature Review

## Purpose

This review expands the Frederick paper beyond its current compact documentary core. The goal is not to bury the paper in secondary reading. It is to give the argument enough historiographical depth and enough archival leads that the next revision can sound like a serious contribution to early republic and local-history scholarship.

The current paper already has a strong spine:

- Wilkinson's direct correspondence with Jefferson and Madison
- the 1804 Frederick Town letter to John Adams
- the 1811 Frederick Town appeal to Madison
- Madison's 1812 notice approving the Frederick-town acquittal

What it lacks is a wider scholarly frame. Right now the bibliography is dominated by Founders Online plus three contextual local items. That is enough for a compact note. It is not enough for a paper that wants to speak with authority about Wilkinson, Jefferson, the Burr crisis, Frederick legal culture, and political survival in the early republic.

## What This Review Changes

The literature falls into five useful clusters:

1. Wilkinson scholarship proper
2. Burr-crisis and early republic political culture
3. Jeffersonian empire, western policy, and military state-building
4. Frederick, Taney, and local legal history
5. Archival and documentary expansion paths

Taken together, these sources point to a stronger paper:

- less dependent on conceptual phrases like "political survivability"
- more grounded in the language of honor, reputation, patronage, and executive management
- more confident about Frederick because the local legal and archival context is thicker
- better able to say what is new about the paper

## A. Highest-Priority Wilkinson Scholarship

These are the first sources to absorb before revising the manuscript.

### 1. Howard W. Cox, *American Traitor: General James Wilkinson's Betrayal of the Republic and Escape from Justice* (Georgetown University Press)

- Link: [Georgetown University Press](https://press.georgetown.edu/Book/American-Traitor)
- Why it matters: this is the most recent major Wilkinson-focused monograph I found. It is also directly interested in the problem your paper cares about: how Wilkinson kept surviving politically and institutionally despite serial scandal.
- Best use in the paper: introduction, relationship section, conclusion
- Likely gain: stronger language about institutional protection, inertia, and executive shielding

### 2. Andro Linklater, *An Artist in Treason: The Extraordinary Double Life of General James Wilkinson* (Walker, 2009)

## How the New Literature Should Change the Paper

### 1. Rewrite the historiography

Right now the paper has a sharp argument but only a thin scholarly conversation behind it. The introduction should name at least three existing ways of writing Wilkinson:

- Wilkinson as traitor and intriguer
- Wilkinson as Burr-crisis functionary inside a larger national panic
- Wilkinson as a case in early republican honor, reputation, and institutional protection

Then the paper can say what it adds:

- not a rehabilitation
- not a new biography
- a Frederick-centered argument about where and how survival became procedural

### 2. Replace some modern abstract nouns with period-relevant language

The current phrases "political survivability" and "procedural redemption" are not wrong, but they are a little synthetic. Freeman, Kierner, Lewis, and the Wilkinson biographies should let the paper speak more concretely about:

- honor
- slander
- vindication
- executive backing
- official settlement
- reputation
- legal defense

### 3. Strengthen the Frederick Barracks paragraph or reduce it

The present barracks claim is plausible, but it is under-sourced. Unless a better local or military-history source is added, the paper should either:

- tighten the claim and make it more modest, or
- replace the paragraph with stronger legal/procedural Frederick evidence

### 4. Make the Taney claim archival, not just remembered

The Taney sentence is currently the most obvious place where the paper feels suggestive instead of solid. The next real gain would come from checking the Taney legal papers and Frederick collections for:

- correspondence
- fee records
- local case paperwork
- court references
- any Wilkinson-related trace

### 5. Bring in one or two Burr-era newspapers

The paper would benefit from a short paragraph or note on how Wilkinson was being discussed publicly, especially around accusation and vindication. That would help connect the private letters to the public atmosphere.

## Recommended Revision Order

If we want the best use of time, I would revise in this order:

1. Read Cox, Lewis, Freeman, Onuf, and one Burr biography.
2. Search the Taney and Frederick archival leads for anything directly tied to Wilkinson's defense.
3. Add one paragraph of historiography to the introduction.
4. Rebuild the Frederick section around documentary scenes, not just interpretive categories.
5. Trim the computation section once the documentary and historiographical sections are stronger.
