\section{Introduction}

Legged robots already adapt well to terrain, payload, and contact variation when given enough sensing and online adaptation machinery \citep{kumar2021rma}. A second line of work shows that energy-aware objectives can materially reshape locomotion behavior \citep{fu2021gaits,aboufazeli2023online}. Meanwhile, efficient test-time adaptation (TTA) has become credible on memory- and latency-constrained hardware \citep{hong2023mecta,jia2024tinytta,xiao2026architecture}. What remains underexplored is the joint question: when a battery-constrained robot must spend energy both to move and to adapt, when is adaptation worth the cost?

This repository still does not ship a full quadruped codebase or hardware benchmark, but it now does ship an executable deployment simulator that makes the scheduling question concrete. The simulator is intentionally lightweight. It tracks a latent disturbance state, a forward-only adaptation estimate, battery depletion, adaptation latency, falls, distance, and compute energy. That is enough to turn the hypothesis into a real computation-backed study rather than a hand-authored demo artifact.

The resulting benchmark compares four policies across terrain and payload shift under both low- and high-battery regimes: a static controller, periodic adaptation, always-on adaptation, and a battery-gated forward-only adaptation rule. The key pattern is a regime-dependent tradeoff. In low-battery regimes, the gated policy improves mission return over always-on adaptation while consuming much less adaptation-compute energy. In high-battery regimes, the learned threshold relaxes into effectively always-on behavior, which supports a scheduling interpretation rather than a universal superiority claim.

This framing matters for two reasons. First, it is a sharper and more falsifiable question than ``do TTA for quadrupeds.'' Second, it matches the repository's advertised end state: the pipeline should be able to leave behind not only idea notes but also a narrative handoff, a plan, code, data, figures, review artifacts, and a final paper package. In that sense, the contribution of this sample is partly methodological. It shows what a complete local artifact trail can look like while still grounding the final manuscript in real executable computation.

Our contributions are:
\begin{itemize}
\item a battery-aware adaptation scheduling hypothesis that emerges naturally from the earlier literature and idea-review stages;
\item a lightweight stochastic deployment simulator that makes the low-battery versus high-battery tradeoff executable and reproducible;
\item a complete paper artifact tree, ending in code, episode-level data, generated figures, a compiled PDF, and a scored review.
\end{itemize}
