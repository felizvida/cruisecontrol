\section{Results}

\input{figures/TABLE_main_results.tex}

The main result is the low-battery tradeoff. In terrain shift at $25\%$ battery, battery-gated adaptation reaches a mission return of $0.56$, ahead of always-on adaptation at $0.54$, while using only $0.15$ Wh of adaptation-compute energy instead of $0.41$ Wh. Payload shift shows the same pattern even more clearly: $0.63$ versus $0.60$, with compute cost reduced from $0.42$ Wh to $0.07$ Wh. Averaged across the two low-battery regimes, the gated rule improves mission return by $0.025$ while cutting adaptation-compute energy by roughly $0.31$ Wh relative to always-on adaptation.

High-battery regimes tell the other half of the story. Under terrain and payload shift at $80\%$ battery, the tuned gating rule adapts at effectively every step and matches always-on adaptation at $0.99$ mission return. That is useful rather than inconvenient: it shows that the scheduler can collapse toward aggressive adaptation when battery pressure is low, instead of insisting on sparse updates everywhere.

\input{figures/TABLE_cost_sweep.tex}

The adaptation-cost sweep sharpens the story. When the adaptation-cost scale is $0.25$, battery-gated and always-on adaptation are effectively tied at $0.57$. As cost rises to $1.0$ and $1.5$, the gated policy moves slightly ahead at $0.56$ versus $0.54$ and $0.53$ versus $0.52$, respectively. At the most extreme cost scale of $2.0$, however, the fixed threshold becomes too aggressive and battery-gated performance falls to $0.46$ while always-on remains at $0.50$. The sweep therefore supports the scheduling claim, but it also exposes a limitation: a single tuned threshold is not robust across arbitrarily large compute-cost shifts.

\input{figures/TABLE_ablation.tex}

The ablation indicates that the full gated rule benefits from both battery state and the compute-cost penalty. Removing either signal drops mission return to $0.53$--$0.54$ and nearly triples compute draw from $0.15$ Wh to $0.41$ Wh. A simple slip-threshold heuristic is cheap, but it also leaves return on the table. Taken together, these results suggest that the benefit is not just sparsity; it is battery-aware sparsity.
