\section{Discussion and Limitations}

The simulator supports a narrow but coherent claim: once adaptation has a non-negligible energy and latency cost, the best adaptation schedule can depend on the deployment regime. That is the most useful scientific signal in the sample. It also aligns with the review pressure from the earlier workflow stages, which repeatedly pushed the project away from ``invent a new TTA method'' and toward ``show a regime where adaptation scheduling matters.'' The right reading of the result is therefore not ``battery-gated adaptation is the new default,'' but ``battery-aware scheduling is a variable the deployment story must explain.''

At the same time, the limitations remain substantial. This is still a lightweight stochastic simulator rather than a physics-realistic locomotion benchmark. The shift process is stylized, the reward is hand-shaped, and the compute-cost model is simple. The cost sweep also exposes a real weakness: a single global threshold is not robust when the adaptation-cost scale moves too far from the tuning distribution. A stronger follow-on project would therefore need at least three steps: instrument a real or simulated locomotion stack, learn or adapt the threshold online instead of fixing it once, and validate the same tradeoff across multiple disturbance families.

Those limitations matter, but they are no longer an excuse for missing evidence. This paper now includes real code, real generated metrics, real figures, and a rebuildable artifact package. It should still be read as a small simulation study rather than as definitive robotics evidence, but the evidence that is present is now executable and inspectable.
