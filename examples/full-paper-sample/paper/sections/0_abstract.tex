Adaptation helps legged robots survive distribution shift, but adaptation itself consumes battery, latency, and onboard compute. This sample paper asks a deployment question: when battery is limited, should a quadruped adapt continuously, or should adaptation be treated as a resource-budgeted action? We instantiate that question on a synthetic benchmark that exists to exercise the full end-to-end workflow in this repository. The benchmark compares four policies: a static controller, periodic adaptation, always-on adaptation, and a battery-gated forward-only adaptation rule. In the low-battery terrain regime, the gated policy improves mission return from $0.67$ to $0.75$ relative to always-on adaptation while reducing adaptation-compute energy from $0.56$ Wh to $0.13$ Wh. In the low-battery payload regime, mission return improves from $0.69$ to $0.77$ with a similar reduction in compute cost. Averaged across the two low-battery regimes, the gated policy gains $0.08$ mission return over always-on adaptation while using less than one third of its adaptation-compute energy. In high-battery regimes, always-on adaptation remains competitive, which points to a scheduling claim rather than a universal superiority claim. The main value of this artifact is twofold: it illustrates a credible battery-aware adaptation hypothesis, and it leaves a compiled paper that demonstrates the intended output shape of the repository's fully automatic research pipeline.
