# Classic Biology Prose

Apply this style when writing or revising: **$ARGUMENTS**

## Aim

Write like a careful researcher speaking to other serious readers:

- clear without sounding simplified
- elegant without sounding ornamental
- confident without sounding inflated
- personal in rhythm, not in self-display
- reflective when stepping back, but still exact

The target voice is shaped by five habits:

- **Monod**: clean, precise sentences
- **Jacob**: a paper that moves like a story
- **Brenner**: intelligence with a little human presence
- **Stent**: reflective prose that can say why the result matters
- **Crick**: conceptual clarity stated early

## Use This By Default When

- drafting a paper from notes or a plan
- rewriting a paper that sounds machine-made, defensive, or procedural
- revising abstract, introduction, discussion, or conclusion
- turning a public-data analysis into a real knowledge claim

## Core Rules

1. Start with the scientific or historical question, not with the workflow.
2. State the central point early.
3. Prefer short to medium declarative sentences.
4. Let the evidence carry the claim. Do not narrate the act of arguing.
5. Give the paper a clear arc: question, difficulty, finding, significance.
6. End with a reflective conclusion, not a generic promise of future work.
7. Write as an authoritative paper, not as a project report, homework note, lab log, or workflow diary.

## What To Avoid

Avoid machine-sounding framing such as:

- "This paper argues..."
- "We do not claim..."
- "small reproducible re-analysis"
- "artifact package"
- "workflow-driven"
- "the manuscript was rewritten to..."
- "this study showcases..."
- "the source paper"
- "the released table"
- "the deposited record"

Avoid defensive prose that keeps explaining what the paper is not. State what the paper shows, then mark limits plainly and briefly.

Avoid project-report habits such as:

- explaining what round of revision produced the text
- narrating file provenance when the reader needs biology
- sounding like a memo to collaborators rather than a paper to the field
- writing sentences that justify the workflow instead of advancing the argument

Avoid inflated prose such as:

- groundbreaking
- revolutionary
- paradigm-shifting
- compellingly demonstrates

Avoid filler transitions such as:

- it is worth noting that
- notably
- importantly
- in this section we

## Sentence-Level Habits

- Use concrete nouns and active verbs.
- Keep the subject close to the verb.
- Replace stacked abstractions with one crisp statement.
- Vary sentence length, but do not let paragraphs become breathless.
- Do not begin too many sentences with "This" or "We".
- If a sentence sounds like it is explaining the paper's workflow, rewrite it around the finding instead.

## Section Guidance

### Abstract

- Open with the biological or historical problem.
- Name the finding by the second or third sentence.
- Include one or two concrete results.
- End with significance, not self-description.

### Introduction

- First paragraph: what is the question and why does it matter.
- Second movement: what is missing or unclear.
- Third movement: what this work establishes.
- Contributions should read like discoveries or clarifications, not software tasks.

### Results

- Lead each subsection with the point of the subsection.
- Move from observation to interpretation without overexplaining.
- Keep the strongest result visible.

### Discussion

- Step back and interpret, but stay close to the evidence.
- Acknowledge limits plainly in one calm paragraph.
- Use this section to say why the result belongs in the broader knowledge base.

### Conclusion

- End with the paper's durable insight.
- One reflective sentence is enough if it is earned.
- Do not close with empty future-work language.

## Rewrite Test

Before accepting a section, ask:

1. Could a human scholar have written this without embarrassment?
2. Does the first paragraph sound like a real paper rather than a system report?
3. If I remove phrases about the paper's process, does the argument get stronger?
4. Does the conclusion leave the reader with an idea, not a workflow summary?
5. Would a strong scientist sound comfortable signing this sentence as part of the paper itself?

If the answer to any of these is no, rewrite.
